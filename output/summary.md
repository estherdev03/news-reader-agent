A ceasefire between Thailand and Cambodia took effect on 27 December 2025 along their disputed border after weeks of intense fighting that displaced nearly one million civilians. The defence ministers of both countries agreed to freeze front lines, ban reinforcements, facilitate the return of displaced civilians, and remove landmines. The deal followed extensive diplomatic pressure from China and the United States. ### 📚 Comprehensive Summary  
The ceasefire, effective from noon on 27 December 2025, marks the latest attempt to halt hostilities between Thailand and Cambodia that have raged for almost three weeks along their contested border area. The conflict has displaced nearly one million people, causing a severe humanitarian crisis. With origins dating back over a century, the border dispute intensified in early 2025 when patriotic acts near contested temples inflamed nationalist tensions. May saw a fatal clash killing a Cambodian soldier, followed by a five-day intense fighting in July that killed dozens of soldiers and civilians and displaced thousands.

Previous ceasefire agreements, including one brokered by US President Donald Trump and Malaysia in October, failed mainly due to violations and mutual mistrust. Thailand had suspended the October truce due to landmine injuries to its soldiers. Diplomatic efforts led by the US and China helped broker the current ceasefire. It includes freezing current front-line positions, banning troop reinforcements, allowing displaced civilians to return home safely, and clearing landmines. Critically, it mandates the release of 18 Cambodian soldiers held by Thailand since July after 72 hours of ceasefire.

However, challenges remain. Thailand expressed skepticism about Cambodia’s commitment, with Defence Minister Natthaphon Narkphanit emphasizing Thailand’s right to self-defense should violations occur. Clashes and air strikes have continued sporadically, with Cambodia condemning Thai air strikes as indiscriminate attacks on civilian areas, highlighting the bitter mistrust between the sides. Nationalist feelings run high in both countries, particularly in Cambodia, which suffered both personnel and equipment losses and retreat from positions held for years.

International observers including the UN human rights chief Volker Türk and an EU spokesperson voiced hopes that the ceasefire will pave the way for lasting peace and urged all parties to act in good faith. Ultimately, the ceasefire represents a delicate pause in a long-standing dispute marred by historical grievances, political nationalism, and military confrontation. Its durability depends on the political will of both nations to move beyond past grievances and uphold peace.
